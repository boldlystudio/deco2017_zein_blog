When we started building BakeLab, the core idea was simple. Sourdough bakers do not need another place to post photos. They need a diagnosis page — somewhere to log the variables, ask a specific question, and get typed, confidence-rated critique from people who have seen the same problem before. That framing shaped every decision from the data schema to the UI.

As team leader, I set up the repository and owned the core technical architecture: the Mojo.js controller logic, database schema, and model layer. I built the Community Feed, Bake Detail, and New Bake pages, implemented photo upload, and led the accessibility audit and fixes. My teammates Belle and Ahmad Zairin contributed the Starter Log, My Bakes pages, and weather API integration, and both ran the user testing sessions. Our Lighthouse audits and usability evidence overlap because we tested the same application. The written reflection here is my own.

# Performance

I ran Lighthouse on all five major pages after deploying to Render. The results:

| Page | Performance | Accessibility | Best Practices | SEO |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Community Feed | 89 | 94 | 100 | 91 |
| Bake Detail | 94 | 94 | 100 | 90 |
| New Bake | 94 | 94 | 100 | 90 |
| My Bakes | 94 | 94 | 100 | 91 |
| Starter Log | 95 | 95 | 100 | 90 |

*Table 1. Lighthouse audit results across BakeLab pages.*

Best Practices hitting 100 across every page was reassuring. No deprecated APIs, no mixed content, clean JavaScript throughout. The HTMX partial rendering approach — comment submissions update the DOM without a full page reload — produced zero Total Blocking Time and zero Cumulative Layout Shift on every page.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-community-npmfeed-lighthouse-testing-updated.png" alt="Community Feed Lighthouse audit showing overall scores">
  <p class="image-caption">Community Feed Lighthouse audit results after deployment to Render.</p>
</div>

The gap between the Community Feed (89) and the Starter Log (95) comes down to images. The feed loads user-uploaded bake photos for every card in the grid. The Starter Log has none. Lighthouse flagged estimated savings of 950ms from document request latency and 128KB from image delivery on the Feed specifically. We did not implement lazy loading, so all feed images load simultaneously on page entry. That explains the Feed's Speed Index of 2.0s compared to the Starter Log's 1.8s.

The consistent accessibility flag — background and foreground colours not meeting sufficient contrast — appears across all pages. I address this below. The SEO flag is simpler: none of our pages have a meta description. That is a straightforward omission that would take about ten minutes to fix with a layout update.

# User Experience and Accessibility

We tested with five participants: Alex (beginner baker), Tia (expert), Mia (casual hobby baker), Sen (casual), and Zac (beginner). The testing covered four task flows: logging a bake, sharing it for community feedback, posting a critique, and using the starter log.

Navigation worked. Every participant completed every core task. 100% found the Community Feed and Starter Log without assistance. Mia said the Community page was easy to find. Tia noted the filters were intuitive once visible.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-usability-testing-updated.png" alt="User testing session overview">
  <p class="image-caption">Overview of user testing sessions conducted across five participants.</p>
</div>

The structured feedback system was the part users responded to most positively. Alex said the confidence levels "made me feel comfortable giving advice." Zac called it the "best part of the app for experienced bakers." Alex also said it "reduces anxiety about giving wrong advice." That was the design intent, and it does not always land. Here it did. What surprised me was that the confidence rating was helping beginners feel safe to participate, not just helping experts calibrate their feedback. That distinction matters for how the feature should be communicated.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/usability-findings-and-insights.png" alt="Usability findings and key insights from testing">
  <p class="image-caption">Key usability findings and insights organised from participant testing sessions.</p>
</div>

The Flour Mix field did not land. All five participants reported confusion. Tia asked what AP flour meant. Sen was unsure about the percentage format. Mia said the free text felt inconsistent. Zac found it difficult on mobile. Alex wanted a dropdown. The problem is in the schema: `flour_mix` is stored as plain `TEXT`, with no format enforced. That was a reasonable prototype trade-off — flexible storage, no migration complexity — but it assumed bakers would self-format the field consistently. They do not.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/schema-draft.png" alt="Original schema showing flour_mix as a plain text field">
  <p class="image-caption">Original schema draft showing flour_mix stored as a single text field — the root cause of the usability issue.</p>
</div>

On accessibility: we fixed colour contrast to `#767676` on white, confirmed at 4.54:1 via WebAIM's contrast checker, which passes WCAG AA for normal text. We wrapped radio button groups in `fieldset` and `legend` so screen readers announce "Feedback Category — Crumb, radio button" instead of just "Crumb, radio button." We replaced `display: none` on radio inputs with a clip and opacity approach that keeps them in the accessibility tree. We added a skip navigation link and fixed the heading hierarchy on the Bake Detail page.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/contrast-checker-after.png" alt="WebAIM contrast checker confirming #767676 passes WCAG AA">
  <p class="image-caption">WebAIM contrast checker confirming #767676 on white at 4.54:1, passing WCAG AA for normal text.</p>
</div>

Lighthouse still flags a contrast failure on the deployed version. After investigating, this appears to be residual — some elements were not caught in the initial fix sweep, likely button states or placeholder text. The accessibility scores sitting at 94–95 instead of 100 reflects that. It is not a major failure, but it is not finished either.

# Critical Reflection

Three things went wrong, and each traces to a specific decision.

The `updateSharing` controller method shipped as a stub. It redirected back to the bake detail page without calling the database. The share/unshare button rendered correctly, accepted a click, and did nothing. This happened because we built the frontend first — the form, the route, the template conditional — and left the backend method for later. Later did not happen before submission. The fix is straightforward (a model call matching `bakeId` and `userId`), but the pattern it reveals is less comfortable: we were verifying visual completeness, not functional completeness.

The Flour Mix field is a schema problem disguised as a UX problem. A label change or better placeholder will not fix it. The fix requires replacing the `TEXT` column with structured inputs — a flour type selector, a percentage field, an Add Flour button — and migrating existing data. We did not test the data entry pattern early enough. One round of testing before schema lock would have caught this.

The colour contrast issue shows a gap in the review process. We fixed the problem in the CSS file but did not verify every context where those colour values appeared. Running axe DevTools on the deployed URL — not just the local build — would have surfaced the remaining failures before submission.

If I were to prioritise fixes: the sharing stub first, because it is a broken core feature; then the Flour Mix field, because it is the most-reported usability failure and requires the most planning to fix properly; then a full axe audit on the live deployment.

# Retrospective on Requirements

Going back to the original Data Definition Document, we planned four tables and six pages. All of it shipped. The tables — `users`, `bakes`, `comments`, `starter_logs` — match what is in the database. All six pages render and function.

| Requirement | Planned | Delivered | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| User Authentication | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Bake CRUD | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Community Feed | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Starter Logs | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Structured Feedback | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Photo Upload | Yes | Yes | Complete |
| Share/Unshare | Yes | Partial | Backend update not called |

*Table 2. Planned versus delivered functionality.*

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-ddd.png" alt="Original BakeLab Data Definition Document">
  <p class="image-caption">The original Data Definition Document showing the four planned tables, all of which were implemented.</p>
</div>

What the DDD did not anticipate: flash messages for the share/unshare confirmation, empty state handling on the feed (a different message when filters return nothing versus when no bakes exist at all), and the owner/visitor conditional logic on the Bake Detail page. None of these appeared in the original requirements, but all of them were necessary. They were discovered during build, not during planning.

Two requirements were misjudged. The `flour_mix` TEXT field was correctly specified for a prototype but incorrect for actual use — the data entry pattern needed validation before committing to that storage approach. The `updateSharing` route was specified and routed but never fully implemented, so a feature that appeared in the requirements existed only at the surface level.

What I would do differently: add a short usability test to the planning phase, before the schema is written. One session with one participant on a paper prototype would have flagged the Flour Mix confusion at zero cost. By the time we ran testing, the schema was already in production.

The feed, the critique system, and the starter log worked the way we planned them. The diagnosis page concept held up under real use. What I did not expect was that the part users responded to most was not a feature exactly — it was that the app made them feel less anxious about participating. That is harder to specify than a database column, and I am not entirely sure how to replicate it deliberately.
