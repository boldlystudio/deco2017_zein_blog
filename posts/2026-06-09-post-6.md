## Introduction

As BakeLab moved from implementation into evaluation, the focus shifted from building features to understanding how well those features actually worked. Earlier blog posts explored planning, architecture, accessibility, and development decisions. This final reflection evaluates the completed application through performance audits, usability testing, accessibility reviews, and a reassessment of the original project requirements.

When we started building BakeLab, the core idea was simple. Sourdough bakers do not need another place to post photos. They need a diagnosis page. Somewhere to log the variables behind a bake, ask a specific question, and receive structured feedback from people who have experienced similar problems before. That idea shaped almost every decision throughout the project, from the database schema to the user interface.

I established the GitHub repository and contributed to the application's technical architecture, including the database schema, controller logic, Community Feed, Bake Detail, and New Bake pages, photo upload functionality, and accessibility improvements. My teammates Belle and Ahmad Zairin contributed the Starter Log, My Bakes pages, weather API integration, and user testing activities. While the testing evidence overlaps because we evaluated the same application, the reflections presented here focus on my own observations and learning.

## Evaluating Performance

To evaluate BakeLab's technical performance, I conducted Lighthouse audits across the application's five major pages after deployment to Render.

  <p class="image-caption">
    Lighthouse audit results across BakeLab pages.
  </p>
</div>

Best Practices reaching 100 across every page was reassuring. The audits identified no deprecated APIs, mixed content issues, or major implementation concerns. The application also benefited from using HTMX for partial page updates. Rather than reloading entire pages when users submit comments, only the relevant section of the interface is updated. This contributed to zero Total Blocking Time and zero Cumulative Layout Shift across all audited pages, resulting in responsive interactions and stable layouts.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-community-feed-lighthouse-testing-updated.png" alt="Community Feed Lighthouse audit">
  <p class="image-caption">
    Community Feed Lighthouse audit results after deployment to Render.
  </p>
</div>

The largest performance difference occurred between the Community Feed and Starter Log pages. While the Starter Log achieved a performance score of 95, the Community Feed scored 89. After reviewing the Lighthouse report, the cause became clear. The Community Feed loads multiple user-uploaded photographs simultaneously, while the Starter Log contains primarily text-based content.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-community-feed-accessibility-lighthouse-testing.png" alt="Community Feed Lighthouse opportunities">
  <p class="image-caption">
    Lighthouse identifying image delivery and accessibility as the main opportunities for improvement.
  </p>
</div>

Lighthouse identified opportunities to improve image delivery and reduce request latency. Because lazy loading was not implemented, all images load when the page first opens. This explains why the Community Feed performed slightly worse than the other pages.

SEO scores were also slightly lower because the application currently lacks page-level meta descriptions. While this does not affect functionality, it reduces discoverability and highlights a feature that was overlooked during development.

Overall, the audits suggest that the application's technical architecture was appropriate for a prototype. However, the evaluation also revealed that decisions made for development speed, such as local image storage and the absence of lazy loading, created trade-offs that would become more noticeable as the application scales.

## Evaluating User Experience and Accessibility

Technical performance alone does not determine whether an application is successful. Users must also be able to understand the interface, complete tasks efficiently, and feel confident using the system.

To evaluate usability, five participants with varying baking experience completed four tasks:

- Log a bake
- Share a bake with the community
- Leave structured feedback
- Record a starter log

All participants successfully completed the tasks and located key features without assistance. This suggested that the application's navigation and information architecture effectively supported its primary workflows.

The structured feedback system received the most positive responses.

  <p class="image-caption">

One participant explained that the confidence rating "made me feel more comfortable giving advice", while another described it as "the best part of the app". These comments suggest that the feature not only helped readers evaluate feedback quality but also reduced uncertainty around participation.

This finding was particularly interesting because the confidence rating was originally designed to help users judge the reliability of feedback. User testing revealed a second benefit: it encouraged participation by making contributors feel more comfortable sharing their opinions.

In contrast, the largest usability issue involved the Flour Mix field. Participants were unsure how to enter flour types, percentages, and abbreviations. Initially, this appeared to be a user interface problem, but testing revealed a deeper issue.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/schema-draft.png" alt="Schema showing flour_mix field">
  <p class="image-caption">
    Original schema showing flour_mix stored as a single text field.
  </p>
</div>

The database stores `flour_mix` as a free-text field. While this simplified implementation, it relied on users creating consistent formats themselves. Testing showed that this assumption was unrealistic. The issue demonstrated how database design decisions can directly affect usability outcomes.

Accessibility testing also informed several improvements, including skip navigation links, improved heading hierarchy, accessible radio button groups, ARIA labels, and colour contrast improvements.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/contrast-checker-after.png" alt="Contrast checker validation">
  <p class="image-caption">
    WebAIM contrast checker confirming WCAG AA compliant colour contrast.
  </p>
</div>

These changes contributed to accessibility scores between 94 and 95 across the audited pages. However, Lighthouse still identified minor contrast issues on the deployed application, demonstrating that accessibility requires ongoing testing and refinement rather than a one-off review.

## Critical Reflection and Improvement Planning

The evaluation process helped identify not only what went wrong, but also why.

One issue emerged during development of the Share and Unshare workflow. Although the interface appeared complete, early testing revealed that the backend process had not yet been fully connected. The issue was resolved before submission, but it highlighted an important lesson: visual completeness does not guarantee functional completeness. Complete user journeys must be tested rather than assuming that a working interface automatically reflects working functionality.

A more significant technical limitation involved image uploads. Users could successfully upload and view photographs during testing, and file validation behaved as intended. However, uploaded images were stored locally rather than in persistent cloud storage. As a result, images could be lost when the development environment was restarted or redeployed.

This decision was appropriate during development because it reduced implementation complexity and allowed the team to focus on core functionality. However, the evaluation highlighted a scalability limitation. If BakeLab were developed further, uploaded images should be stored using a dedicated service such as Cloudinary or Amazon S3 to ensure persistence across deployments and server restarts.

The Flour Mix field revealed another lesson. The problem could not be solved through better labels or placeholder text because the issue originated in the schema itself. Future versions should replace free-text entry with structured controls such as flour selectors and percentage fields.

Finally, the remaining accessibility issues highlighted a gap in the review process. While colour contrast improvements were tested locally, some issues remained on the deployed application. Running accessibility audits on the live environment earlier would likely have identified these problems sooner.

Based on the findings, my priorities for future development would be:

1. Replace the Flour Mix free-text field with structured inputs.
2. Implement persistent cloud storage for uploaded images.
3. Conduct a comprehensive accessibility audit on the deployed application.
4. Add contextual help for baking terminology.
5. Improve image loading through optimisation and lazy loading.

These recommendations focus on addressing the root causes identified during testing rather than simply adding new features.

## Retrospective Assessment of Functional Requirements

Returning to the original Data Definition Document, most planned functionality was successfully implemented and remained relevant throughout development.

  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/comparison-table.png" alt="Comparison between planned and delivered functionality">
  <p class="image-caption">
    Comparison between planned and delivered functionality.
  </p>
</div>

The original requirements focused on core baking workflows and were generally realistic and appropriately prioritised. However, the project underestimated the importance of accessibility requirements and overestimated the suitability of free-text inputs such as Flour Mix.

Several features also emerged during development that were not anticipated during planning, including flash messages, empty-state handling, owner-specific page behaviour, and additional accessibility requirements. These were discovered through implementation and testing rather than through initial planning.

  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-ddd.png" alt="Original BakeLab Data Definition Document">
  <p class="image-caption">

Looking back, the most important lesson was that requirements are not fixed decisions. They are assumptions that should be revisited as evidence emerges.

The structured critique system was included in the original requirements and remained one of the project's core features throughout development. However, user testing revealed an outcome that was never explicitly anticipated during planning. Participants repeatedly reported feeling more confident contributing feedback because the confidence rating system reduced anxiety about giving incorrect advice.

While the feature was originally designed to help users evaluate the reliability of feedback, it also encouraged participation by making contributors feel more comfortable sharing their opinions. This demonstrated that the value of the feature extended beyond information quality and into user confidence and community engagement.

This finding reinforced an important lesson: while functional requirements can define what a system should do, they do not always predict how people will feel when using it. Some of the most meaningful outcomes only become visible once real users interact with the application.
