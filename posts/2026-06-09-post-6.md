BakeLab was designed to help sourdough bakers diagnose problems, document their baking process, and receive structured feedback. Rather than functioning as a typical social platform, it focused on learning through critique and shared experience.

As team leader, I established the GitHub repository and contributed to the application's architecture, database schema, controller logic, Community Feed, Bake Detail, New Bake pages, photo uploads, and accessibility improvements. My teammates Belle and Ahmad Zairin developed the Starter Log, My Bakes pages, weather API integration, and user testing activities. While testing evidence was shared across the team, this reflection focuses on my own observations and learning.

# Performance

I ran Lighthouse on all five major pages after deploying to Render. The results:

| Page | Performance | Accessibility | Best Practices | SEO |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Community Feed | 89 | 94 | 100 | 91 |
| Bake Detail | 94 | 94 | 100 | 90 |
| New Bake | 94 | 94 | 100 | 90 |
| My Bakes | 94 | 94 | 100 | 91 |
| Starter Log | 95 | 95 | 100 | 90 |

*Table 1. Lighthouse audit results across BakeLab pages.*

A key achievement was a Best Practices score of 100 across all pages, indicating that the application avoided common implementation issues and followed modern web standards.

HTMX also contributed to performance by updating only relevant page sections instead of reloading entire pages. Lighthouse reported zero Total Blocking Time and zero Cumulative Layout Shift, suggesting responsive interactions and stable layouts.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-community-feed-lighthouse-testing-updated.png" alt="Community Feed Lighthouse audit showing overall scores">
  <p class="image-caption">Community Feed Lighthouse audit results after deployment to Render.</p>
</div>

The Community Feed recorded the lowest performance score at 89. Lighthouse identified image loading as the primary cause. Unlike text-heavy pages such as Starter Log, the feed loads multiple user-uploaded photos simultaneously. The report estimated savings from reduced request latency and image optimisation. Because lazy loading was not implemented, all images loaded immediately when the page opened.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-community-feed-accessibility-lighthouse-testing.png" alt="Lighthouse flagging image delivery as the primary performance opportunity">
  <p class="image-caption">Lighthouse flagging image delivery and accessibility as the primary opportunities on the Community Feed.</p>
</div>

SEO scores were slightly lower because pages lacked meta descriptions. While this does not affect functionality, it reduces discoverability and could be addressed easily.

Overall, BakeLab performed well as a prototype, though image optimisation and SEO improvements would be important for future growth.

# User Experience and Accessibility

Performance metrics alone do not reveal whether users can successfully complete tasks. To evaluate usability, five participants with varying baking experience completed four tasks:

- Log a bake
- Share a bake with the community
- Leave structured feedback
- Record a starter log

All participants completed the tasks successfully and located key features without assistance. This suggested that the application's navigation and information architecture supported its primary workflows effectively.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/bakelab-usability-testing-updated.png" alt="User testing session overview">
  <p class="image-caption">Overview of user testing sessions conducted across five participants.</p>
</div>

The structured feedback system received the most positive responses. Participants reported that confidence ratings made them more comfortable contributing advice. Interestingly, the feature not only helped readers judge feedback quality but also reduced anxiety for contributors, encouraging participation.

<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/usability-findings-and-insights.png" alt="Usability findings and key insights from testing">
  <p class="image-caption">Key usability findings and insights organised from participant testing sessions.</p>
</div>

The largest usability issue involved the Flour Mix field. Participants were unsure how to enter flour types, percentages, and abbreviations. Initially, this appeared to be a user interface problem, but testing revealed a deeper issue.

The database stores  `flour_mix` as a free-text `TEXT` field. While this simplified implementation, it relied on users creating consistent formats themselves. Testing showed that this assumption was unrealistic. The issue demonstrated how database design decisions can directly affect usability.


<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/schema-draft.png" alt="Original schema showing flour_mix as a plain text field">
  <p class="image-caption">Original schema draft showing flour_mix stored as a single text field — the root cause of the usability issue.</p>
</div>

Accessibility testing also led to several improvements:

- Skip navigation links
- Improved heading hierarchy
- Accessible radio button groups
- ARIA labels
- WCAG AA colour contrast improvements



<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/contrast-checker-after.png" alt="WebAIM contrast checker confirming #767676 passes WCAG AA">
  <p class="image-caption">WebAIM contrast checker confirming #767676 on white at 4.54:1, passing WCAG AA for normal text.</p>
</div>

These changes contributed to accessibility scores between 94 and 95. However, Lighthouse still identified some contrast issues on the deployed application, highlighting the need for ongoing accessibility testing rather than relying solely on local checks.

# Critical Reflection

The evaluation process helped identify why certain problems occurred and where improvements should be prioritised.

The first issue involved the Share and Unshare workflow. Although the interface appeared complete, parts of the backend functionality were unfinished. This revealed that our team focused more on validating visual behaviour than testing complete end-to-end functionality.

The second issue was the Flour Mix field. Testing showed that better labels alone would not solve the problem. The schema itself requires redesign. Future versions should use structured inputs such as flour selectors and percentage fields instead of free-text entry.

The third issue involved accessibility. Some contrast problems remained after deployment despite local testing. This reinforced the importance of auditing production environments.


<div class="blog-image">
  <img src="/deco2017_zein_blog/assets/images/future-improvements.png" alt="Prioritised improvement areas identified through evaluation">
  <p class="image-caption">Prioritised improvement areas identified through evaluation and user testing.</p>
</div>

Based on the findings, my priorities for future development would be:

1. Replace the Flour Mix free-text field with structured inputs.
2. Complete and thoroughly test the Share and Unshare workflow.
3. Conduct a comprehensive accessibility audit on the deployed application.
4. Add contextual help for baking terminology.
5. Improve image loading through optimisation and lazy loading.

These changes directly address the most significant issues identified during testing.

# Retrospective Assessment of Functional Requirements

The final stage of reflection involved comparing the completed application against the original project requirements.

| Requirement         | Planned | Delivered | Notes                       |
| ------------------- | ------- | --------- | --------------------------- |
| User Authentication | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Bake CRUD           | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Community Feed      | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Starter Logs        | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Structured Feedback | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Photo Upload        | Yes     | Yes       | Complete                    |
| Share / Unshare     | Yes     | Partial   | Requires backend refinement |

*Table 2. Comparison between planned and delivered functionality.*

Most planned functionality was successfully implemented. However, several requirements emerged during development that were not anticipated in the original design, including:

* Flash messages
* Empty-state handling
* Owner-specific page behaviour
* Additional accessibility requirements

These features became necessary only after implementation and testing exposed gaps in the original assumptions.

The project demonstrated that success is not measured solely by delivering planned requirements. User experience also plays a critical role. While the feed, critique system, starter log, and documentation workflow functioned as intended, the most valuable outcome was unexpected. Participants repeatedly reported feeling more confident contributing feedback because of the structured critique system.

Although this benefit was never listed in the original requirements, it became one of the application's strongest contributions.

# Lessons Learned

BakeLab reinforced the importance of validating assumptions throughout development. Several issues identified during testing, particularly the Flour Mix field, originated from decisions that seemed reasonable during implementation but were never evaluated with users beforehand.

What I would do differently is add a short usability test to the planning phase before the schema is written. One session with a single participant using a paper prototype would likely have flagged the Flour Mix confusion at virtually no cost. By the time we conducted usability testing, the schema was already in production, making the issue more difficult to address.

From a technical perspective, the project strengthened my understanding of TypeScript, Mojo.js, SQLite database design, accessibility auditing, Lighthouse evaluation, GitHub workflows, and full-stack development.

The feed, the critique system, and the starter log worked the way we planned them. The diagnosis page concept also held up under real use. What I did not expect was that the part users responded to most was not a feature exactly—it was that the application made them feel less anxious about participating. That outcome is harder to specify than a database column, and I am not entirely sure how to replicate it deliberately.

More importantly, the project highlighted that successful digital products are not defined only by technical quality. They are shaped by how effectively they reduce uncertainty, encourage participation, and help users achieve their goals.
